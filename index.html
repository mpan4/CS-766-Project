Deep Learning for Automated Blood Layer Segmentation in Phlebotomy Training

A hybrid MobileNet + U-Net pipeline that automatically segments plasma, buffy coat, and red blood cells in centrifuged tube images — providing objective, pixel-level feedback for phlebotomy training and clinical quality control.

Michael Pan <mpan4@wisc.edu> University of Wisconsin–Madison Spring 2026
View Code View PowerPoint View PDF

Abstract

Recognizing proper blood separation after centrifugation is critical for specimen quality assessment in clinical laboratories. After centrifugation, blood separates into three distinct layers: plasma, buffy coat, and red blood cells. Current manual training methods suffer from subjectivity, time consumption, and lack of standardized feedback. This work presents an automated system employing a U-Net architecture with semi-automatic annotation using the Segment Anything Model (SAM) to enable real-time blood layer detection. The proposed approach combines semantic segmentation with an active learning-inspired annotation strategy and MobileNet-based region-of-interest localization to reduce annotation burden while maintaining high accuracy. A dataset of over 300 blood tube images, augmented through color perturbations, is used to train the model, and performance is evaluated using pixel-wise accuracy and distance-based metrics between predicted and true layer boundaries.

01 · Motivation

Motivation

Blood layer recognition after centrifugation is a fundamental skill in phlebotomy and clinical laboratory science. Proper identification of the three blood layers — plasma (yellowish-clear top layer), buffy coat (thin white/gray middle layer containing leukocytes and platelets), and red blood cells (dense red bottom layer) — is essential for specimen quality assessment and downstream diagnostic accuracy.

Current training for blood layer recognition relies on manual instruction and qualitative feedback from experienced personnel. This process is subjective and time-consuming, with large variability in trainee performance. There is no standardized, quantitative feedback mechanism for judging whether a trainee has correctly identified the plasma, buffy coat, and red blood cell boundaries in centrifuged tubes. As laboratories scale to support high-throughput screening — especially in oncology — the need for reproducible and objective training tools becomes critical.

Core Problem

Inconsistent or incorrect assessment of layer quality can propagate errors into molecular assays, leading to missed or incorrect diagnoses and increased healthcare costs. An automated, pixel-level segmentation tool provides the objective, standardized feedback that current manual training lacks.

Project Goals

  • Automatically segment the three primary blood layers in images of centrifuged tubes with pixel-level precision
  • Estimate transition heights between plasma–buffy coat and buffy coat–red blood cells
  • Integrate into a phlebotomy training platform that provides immediate, objective feedback to trainees
  • Support standardized quality control in clinical laboratories for high-throughput cancer screening workflows
02 · Approach

Approach

The system combines two complementary deep learning components: a MobileNet-based object detector for region-of-interest localization and a U-Net semantic segmentation model for pixel-wise blood layer classification. This two-stage pipeline addresses the challenge that raw images contain substantial background and variability in tube position, which would degrade segmentation accuracy if handled naively.

Annotation is semi-automated using the Segment Anything Model (SAM): annotators provide point or box prompts on each blood layer and SAM generates initial segmentation masks, which are then refined by human experts. This reduces annotation time substantially compared to fully manual polygon drawing while maintaining label quality — consistent with findings in the literature showing SAM-assisted workflows reduce annotation effort by over 50%.

Pipeline Overview

1

Data Collection & SAM-Assisted Annotation

300+ RGB images of centrifuged blood tubes captured in a production-like environment. VGG Image Annotator (VIA) used for ground-truth labels; SAM used to generate initial pixel masks from point/box prompts, refined by human experts.

2

Data Augmentation

Manual color augmentation — random perturbations in hue, contrast, and saturation — applied to each image prior to training to simulate realistic lighting and imaging variability. Empirically more effective than on-the-fly augmentation alone.

3

MobileNet ROI Localization

A MobileNet-based object detector localizes the blood tube and outputs normalized bounding boxes. A fixed 368×368 crop centered on the detected transition region is extracted, ensuring the U-Net focuses on the anatomically relevant area.

4

U-Net Segmentation

A four-class U-Net (background, plasma, buffy coat, red blood cells) produces pixel-wise segmentation masks. Predicted transition heights are extracted by scanning for class boundaries and compared to expert ground-truth annotations.

03 · Implementation

Implementation

The full pipeline is implemented in TensorFlow/Keras. A custom data generator orchestrates joint loading, detection, preprocessing, and augmentation for each training batch — running the MobileNet detector, normalizing pixel intensities to [0, 1], padding images to 2048px height, and extracting the 368×368 crop centered on the detected transition region.

U-Net Architecture

The segmentation model follows the canonical U-Net encoder–decoder design with modifications for RGB input and four-class output.

  • Encoder: Four downsampling levels with 64, 128, 256, and 512 filters each, followed by max pooling. ReLU activations, same padding, He normal initialization.
  • Bottleneck: Two convolutional layers with 1024 filters and dropout rate 0.5 for regularization.
  • Decoder: Four upsampling levels mirroring the encoder, each concatenating encoder features via skip connections for high-resolution spatial detail.
  • Output: 1×1 convolution with softmax producing a 368×368×4 probability map over the four classes.

Training Setup

  • Loss: Sparse categorical cross-entropy
  • Optimizer: Adam, learning rate \(1 \times 10^{-4}\)
  • Metrics: Pixel-wise sparse categorical accuracy
  • Checkpointing: Best model saved on validation loss, exported as both `.h5` code and frozen TensorFlow graph
  • Input size: 368×368×3 RGB

Key Design Decision: Offline vs. On-the-Fly Augmentation

An earlier object-detection-only approach used standard on-the-fly augmentation, which produced limited robustness to lighting variation. Switching to offline color augmentation — pre-generating multiple copies of each training image with randomized hue/contrast/saturation perturbations — significantly improved generalization, particularly for the thin buffy coat layer where color cues are critical. This is the most important implementation-level finding of the project.

04 · Results

Results

Performance is evaluated on a held-out set of 100 manually annotated images not used during training or validation. For each image, predicted transition heights extracted by scanning the segmentation mask for class boundaries are compared against expert ground-truth annotations. The key metric is average absolute difference in pixels between predicted and true transition heights.

Notable Finding

The buffy–blood cell transition is consistently easier for the model to localize than the plasma–buffy transition across all four variants. Model 2 achieves near-pixel-perfect precision (0.96 px) at the buffy–blood interface, suggesting that the dense red blood cell layer provides a strong color signal that the U-Net leverages reliably.

Qualitative Results

Visual inspection of segmentation outputs confirms that the U-Net reliably delineates plasma and red blood cell regions. The most challenging area is the thin buffy coat, where predicted boundaries closely follow ground-truth transitions even in the presence of varying lighting or partial occlusions from tube labels. The combination of MobileNet-based ROI localization and U-Net segmentation is particularly effective at reducing the influence of background artifacts.

05 · Discussion

Discussion

The proposed hybrid pipeline demonstrates clinically relevant precision in estimating layer boundaries, supporting its potential use in phlebotomy training platforms and standardized laboratory quality control. The combination of SAM-assisted annotation, offline color augmentation, and two-stage detection/segmentation each contributed meaningfully to final performance.

Problems Encountered

Buffy Coat Thickness Variability

The model shows sensitivity to extreme variations in buffy coat thickness. Samples with unusually thin or thick buffy coats produce larger boundary errors, as the model has seen fewer examples of extreme cases during training.

Label & Artifact Occlusions

Occasional errors occur when tube labels or imaging artifacts intersect the layer boundaries, confusing the segmentation model. Manual annotation for these edge cases was particularly time-consuming.

Limited Evaluation Metric

The current evaluation focuses on height differences only. This misses cases where the mask shape is correct but the boundary height estimate deviates, or vice versa. A broader metric suite — Dice, IoU, class-wise precision/recall — would give a more complete picture.

Annotation Bottleneck

Even with SAM assistance, generating high-quality pixel masks for 300 images was labor-intensive, particularly for the thin buffy coat region where SAM’s initial masks required significant manual refinement.

Limitations & Future Work

The current dataset covers one production environment; generalization to other tube types, centrifuge settings, or imaging equipment has not been verified. Future directions include:

  • Expand dataset to additional specimen and tube types to improve generalization
  • Add Dice coefficient, Intersection over Union (IoU), and class-wise precision/recall to the evaluation suite
  • Benchmark against alternative architectures, such as transformer-based segmentation models
  • Deploy in a mobile or web-based interface for point-of-care use in training environments
  • Integrate a full active learning loop, since the pipeline is already designed to be compatible with continuous refinement as new data are collected
  • Incorporate Dice loss or boundary-aware losses to further emphasize accurate layer transition localization
